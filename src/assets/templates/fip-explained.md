# Understanding Feline Infectious Peritonitis (FIP)

> **Purpose:** FIP basics explained in client-friendly language
> **Audience:** Pet owners whose cats have been diagnosed with or suspected of having FIP
> **Tone:** Compassionate, clear, non-technical

---

## What You Need to Know First

If your cat has been diagnosed with FIP, or your veterinarian suspects FIP, you likely have many questions and concerns. This guide explains FIP in straightforward terms and, most importantly, provides **hope** - because FIP is no longer the uniformly fatal disease it once was.

**The most important message:** With modern antiviral treatment, **more than 85% of cats with FIP can be cured**.

---

## What is FIP?

### The Simple Explanation

**Feline Infectious Peritonitis (FIP)** is a disease caused by a specific type of virus that affects cats. Despite the name, it's not primarily an infection of the peritoneum (abdominal lining) - it's a whole-body disease that can affect many organs.

### What Causes FIP?

FIP develops through an unfortunate chain of events:

1. **Nearly all cats are exposed to feline coronavirus** (FCoV)
   - This is an extremely common, usually harmless virus
   - 40-80% of cats worldwide carry it
   - Most cats with FCoV have no symptoms or just mild diarrhea
   - It's spread through feces, grooming, and sharing litter boxes

2. **In rare cases, the coronavirus mutates**
   - In about 5-10% of infected cats, the virus mutates inside the cat's body
   - This mutation changes a harmless virus into one that causes FIP
   - The mutation happens randomly - it's not something you could have prevented
   - The mutated virus is called FIPV (FIP virus)

3. **The mutated virus causes disease**
   - The cat's immune system tries to fight the virus
   - Paradoxically, the immune response itself causes much of the damage
   - This leads to inflammation in various organs
   - Without treatment, the disease progresses

**Important:** Your cat did not "catch FIP" from another cat. The mutation happens inside each individual cat. While cats can spread the common coronavirus to each other, they cannot directly transmit FIP.

## Why Do Some Cats Get FIP?

### Risk Factors

We don't fully understand why some cats develop FIP while most don't, but certain factors increase risk:

**Age:**
- **Kittens and young cats** (under 2 years) are most commonly affected
- Senior cats (over 10 years) are also at higher risk
- Cats between 3-10 years can get FIP but it's less common

**Living Situation:**
- Cats from shelters, catteries, or multi-cat households
- Crowded conditions with shared litter boxes
- Stressful environments

**Genetics:**
- Some cat breeds appear more susceptible (Bengals, Ragdolls, Birmans)
- Purebred cats may be at slightly higher risk than mixed breeds

**Stress:**
- Recent surgery (like spaying/neutering)
- Moving to a new home
- Introduction of new pets
- Other illnesses

**Important:** None of these factors mean you did anything wrong. FIP can happen to any cat, even well-cared-for cats in loving homes.

## Types of FIP

FIP appears in different forms, though cats can have features of more than one type:

### Wet (Effusive) FIP

**Most common form** (~70% of cases)

- Fluid accumulates in body cavities (abdomen and/or chest)
- Pot-bellied appearance if fluid in abdomen
- Difficulty breathing if fluid in chest
- Often progresses rapidly
- **Good news:** Usually responds very well to treatment

### Dry (Non-Effusive) FIP

- No fluid accumulation, or only small amounts
- Inflammation damages specific organs
- Can affect kidneys, liver, eyes, brain, intestines
- Symptoms depend on which organs are affected
- May progress more slowly than wet FIP
- More challenging to diagnose

### Neurological FIP

- Affects the brain and/or spinal cord
- Seizures, behavior changes, wobbling, head tilt
- May occur alone or with other FIP forms
- Requires higher treatment doses
- Many cats still respond well to treatment

### Ocular (Eye) FIP

- Affects one or both eyes
- Cloudiness, color changes, inflammation
- May occur alone or with other FIP forms
- Can respond well to treatment
- Some vision changes may be permanent even after cure

## How is FIP Diagnosed?

### Why Diagnosis is Challenging

There is **no single definitive test** for FIP (except biopsy/necropsy). Veterinarians must piece together multiple clues:

**Tests that help diagnose FIP:**
- Physical examination findings
- Blood tests (especially protein levels)
- Fluid analysis (if wet FIP)
- Imaging (ultrasound, X-rays)
- Eye examination

**Tests that are often misunderstood:**
- **Coronavirus antibody tests:** Only show exposure to coronavirus, not FIP
  - Most cats test positive but don't have FIP
  - Not useful for diagnosis
- **Coronavirus PCR tests:** Can support diagnosis but not definitive
  - Positive result doesn't confirm FIP
  - Negative result doesn't rule out FIP

Your veterinarian uses a **diagnostic approach** that combines multiple findings to assess the likelihood of FIP.

## FIP Used to Be Fatal - What Changed?

### The Old Reality (Before 2019)

- FIP was considered 100% fatal
- No effective treatments existed
- Supportive care could only temporarily improve quality of life
- Most cats survived only weeks to months after diagnosis
- Euthanasia was often recommended

### The Breakthrough (2019-Present)

**Antiviral medications changed everything:**

- **GS-441524** (most commonly used)
- **Remdesivir** (similar drug)

These medications were developed for treating coronaviruses in humans and were found to be effective against the FIP virus.

**Research from UC Davis Veterinary School showed:**
- Over 85% cure rate in cats with wet FIP
- 70-75% cure rate in cats with dry FIP
- 60-70% cure rate in cats with neurological FIP
- Many cats with ocular FIP also respond

**This is a genuine medical breakthrough** - transforming a fatal disease into a curable one.

## How is FIP Treated?

### The Treatment Protocol

**Medication:**
- Daily injections or oral medication of GS-441524
- Dose depends on disease type and severity
- Given at home by pet owners (you can learn!)

**Duration:**
- Minimum 12 weeks (84 days) of treatment
- Many cats need 14-16 weeks or longer
- Treatment continues until cat is clinically well and bloodwork normalizes

**Monitoring:**
- Weekly weight checks
- Monthly bloodwork
- Regular veterinary examinations
- Tracking of symptoms

**What to Expect:**
- Most cats start improving within 1-2 weeks
- Appetite returns quickly
- Energy increases
- Fever resolves
- Fluid decreases (wet FIP)
- Complete recovery takes several months

---

## Success Rates and Prognosis

### The Good News

**Most cats with FIP can be cured** with proper treatment:

- **85%+ success rate** for wet FIP
- **70-75% success rate** for dry FIP
- **60-70% success rate** for neurological FIP
- Many cats with ocular FIP also respond

**Factors that improve success:**
- Starting treatment early (before severe organ damage)
- Young, otherwise healthy cats
- Owner ability to commit to full treatment course
- Consistent, daily medication
- Proper dosing

### The Challenges

**Treatment is demanding:**
- Daily medication for 3-4+ months
- Financial investment (medication costs vary by region)
- Emotional commitment during treatment
- Monitoring and veterinary visits
- Dealing with injections (if using injectable form)

**Not all cats respond:**
- 15-30% of cats don't achieve cure
- Some cats relapse after initial improvement
- Neurological cases can be more challenging
- Cats with severe organ damage may not recover

---

## What About Relapse?

### Relapse is Possible

- **5-10% of cats relapse** after stopping treatment
- Usually happens within 3-6 months after treatment ends
- Most relapses can be treated successfully with another course
- Post-treatment monitoring helps catch relapses early

### Reducing Relapse Risk

- Complete the full treatment course (don't stop early!)
- Follow your veterinarian's tapering protocol if used
- Continue monitoring after treatment ends
- Watch for returning symptoms
- Maintain regular veterinary care

## Living with a Cat Being Treated for FIP

### Daily Life During Treatment

**Your cat can still enjoy life:**
- Play and interact normally
- Live with other cats (FIP itself isn't contagious)
- Eat favorite foods
- Receive love and attention

**What you'll need to do:**
- Give daily injections or oral medication
- Monitor appetite and energy
- Track weight weekly
- Watch for side effects
- Keep veterinary appointments
- Maintain treatment log

**Most owners report:**
- The routine becomes manageable after first few weeks
- Seeing improvement makes it worthwhile
- Support from FIP communities is invaluable
- The effort is worth it for their cat's life

### Other Cats in the Home

**Good news:**
- Your other cats are not at risk of catching FIP
- The FIP-causing mutation can't be transmitted
- Your treated cat can continue living with other cats

**However:**
- They may share the common coronavirus (which is okay)
- Reduce stress during treatment period
- Ensure clean litter boxes (scoop 2x daily)
- Feed separately if food competition causes stress

## Financial Considerations

### Cost of Treatment

**Medication costs vary significantly:**
- Depends on cat's weight and disease type
- Ranges from affordable to expensive depending on source and region
- Many owners find ways to make treatment affordable
- Complete treatment may cost several hundred to several thousand dollars

**Your veterinarian can provide:**
- Cost estimates specific to your cat
- Information about medication sources
- Payment plan options
- Guidance on financial resources

**Remember:**
- Cost is a valid concern - discuss openly with your veterinarian
- Some owners find creative solutions (fundraising, payment plans)
- Quality of life considerations are important
- There's no judgment in making decisions based on your situation

## Common Misconceptions About FIP

### Myth vs. Reality

**MYTH:** "FIP is contagious to other cats"
- **REALITY:** The common coronavirus is contagious, but FIP itself is not. The mutation happens inside each individual cat.

**MYTH:** "A positive coronavirus test means my cat has FIP"
- **REALITY:** Most cats test positive for coronavirus but don't have FIP. This test is not useful for diagnosis.

**MYTH:** "FIP is always fatal"
- **REALITY:** This was true before 2019, but modern antiviral treatment can cure most cats with FIP.

**MYTH:** "Treatment is experimental"
- **REALITY:** GS-441524 has been used successfully in thousands of cats worldwide since 2019.

**MYTH:** "If my cat has FIP, it's because I did something wrong"
- **REALITY:** FIP is not caused by anything you did or didn't do. It's a random mutation that happens unpredictably.

**MYTH:** "All cats with FIP will relapse"
- **REALITY:** Only 5-10% of successfully treated cats relapse, and most relapses can be treated successfully.

## Questions to Ask Your Veterinarian

### Important Discussions

1. **About Diagnosis:**
   - How confident are you this is FIP?
   - What findings support FIP diagnosis?
   - What else could it be? (differential diagnoses)
   - Do we need more tests?

2. **About Treatment:**
   - Do you recommend treating my cat?
   - What is the expected success rate for my cat's situation?
   - What dose and duration do you recommend?
   - How will we monitor response?

3. **About Medication:**
   - Injectable or oral form?
   - Where do I get the medication?
   - How do I store and handle it?
   - Can you teach me injection technique?

4. **About Prognosis:**
   - What can I realistically expect?
   - When should we see improvement?
   - What are signs treatment isn't working?
   - How will we know when to stop treatment?

5. **About Cost:**
   - What is estimated total cost?
   - Do you offer payment plans?
   - What are my options if cost is prohibitive?
