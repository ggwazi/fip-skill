# Relapse Watch: Post-Treatment Monitoring Guide

> **Purpose:** Post-treatment monitoring guidance for preventing and detecting FIP relapse
> **Audience:** Pet owners whose cats have completed FIP treatment
> **Timeline:** Post-treatment (after 84+ days of treatment)

---

## Congratulations!

Your cat has completed FIP treatment! This is a major milestone. Most cats who complete treatment remain healthy for life. However, a small percentage (5-10%) may experience relapse, usually within 3-6 months after stopping treatment.

**This guide will help you:**
- Understand what relapse is and why it happens
- Know what signs to watch for
- Follow an appropriate monitoring schedule
- Know when to contact your veterinarian
- Feel confident in your cat's long-term health

---

## Understanding Relapse

### What Is Relapse?

**Relapse** means FIP returns after treatment is stopped. The virus was not completely eliminated and begins replicating again.

**Important Facts:**
- **Only 5-10% of successfully treated cats relapse**
- **Most relapses occur within 3-6 months** after stopping treatment
- **Late relapses** (beyond 6 months) can happen but are less common
- **Most relapses respond well to retreatment** (80-85% success rate)

### Why Does Relapse Happen?

We don't fully understand why some cats relapse while others don't. Possible factors:

- **Treatment stopped too soon** (before virus fully eliminated)
- **Viral sanctuaries** (virus hiding in certain tissues)
- **Individual immune response** variation
- **Neurological or ocular FIP** (higher relapse risk than wet FIP)
- **Underdosing during treatment** (due to weight gain, compliance issues)

**What relapse is NOT:**
- ❌ Not your fault
- ❌ Not catching FIP again from another cat
- ❌ Not inevitable (90-95% do NOT relapse)

### Which Cats Are at Higher Risk?

**Higher Relapse Risk:**
- Neurological FIP (10-15% relapse rate)
- Ocular FIP (10-15% relapse rate)
- Treatment stopped early (before bloodwork normalized)
- Underdosed during treatment
- Very young kittens when treated

**Lower Relapse Risk:**
- Wet FIP (5-7% relapse rate)
- Dry FIP (5-10% relapse rate)
- Complete treatment course (12-16+ weeks)
- Bloodwork fully normalized before stopping
- Older kittens and young adult cats

## Monitoring Schedule

### First 3 Months Post-Treatment (Highest Risk Period)

**Home Monitoring (Weekly):**

✓ **Weight Check:**
- Weigh your cat once weekly
- Use the same scale, same time of day
- Record weight
- **Watch for:** Weight loss >5% in 2 weeks

✓ **Appetite Assessment:**
- Is your cat eating normally?
- Any decrease in interest in food?
- **Watch for:** Decreased appetite lasting >2 days

✓ **Energy Level:**
- Is your cat playful and active?
- Normal sleep patterns?
- **Watch for:** Increased lethargy, sleeping more

✓ **Temperature (Optional):**
- Not necessary for all cats
- Consider if cat had persistent fever during FIP
- **Normal:** 100.5-102.5°F (38-39°C)
- **Watch for:** Fever >102.5°F (39°C) lasting >24 hours

**Veterinary Monitoring:**

**Month 1 Post-Treatment:**
- Physical examination
- Bloodwork (CBC, chemistry panel)
- **Purpose:** Ensure bloodwork remains normal

**Month 3 Post-Treatment:**
- Physical examination
- Bloodwork (CBC, chemistry panel)
- **Purpose:** Confirm continued remission

**If All Normal:**
- Very low relapse risk at this point
- Can extend monitoring interval

### Months 4-6 Post-Treatment

**Home Monitoring (Biweekly):**
- Weight check every 2 weeks
- Daily appetite/energy observation
- Report any changes to veterinarian

**Veterinary Monitoring:**

**Month 6 Post-Treatment:**
- Physical examination
- Bloodwork (CBC, chemistry panel)
- **Purpose:** Final routine check for most cats

**If All Normal:**
- Relapse risk very low after 6 months
- Return to annual wellness exams for most cats

### Beyond 6 Months Post-Treatment

**Home Monitoring:**
- General wellness observation (as with any cat)
- Weight check monthly or as part of routine care
- Watch for any unusual symptoms

**Veterinary Monitoring:**

**Month 12 Post-Treatment:**
- Annual wellness exam
- Bloodwork (consider including A:G ratio)
- **Purpose:** Comprehensive health check

**After 12 Months:**
- Annual wellness exams
- FIP relapse very unlikely at this point
- Your cat is considered cured!

## Signs of Relapse to Watch For

### Early Warning Signs

**Relapse often starts subtly:**

✓ **Decreased Appetite**
- Eating less than usual
- Taking longer to finish meals
- Showing less interest in favorite foods

✓ **Reduced Energy**
- More time sleeping or resting
- Less playful
- Withdrawing from family

✓ **Weight Loss**
- Gradual decline over 2-4 weeks
- Losing muscle mass
- Ribs becoming more prominent

✓ **Mild Fever**
- May or may not be present
- Often comes and goes
- Cat may feel warm to touch

**Important:** These signs can have many causes (not just FIP relapse). Always consult your veterinarian, but don't panic.

### Disease-Specific Relapse Signs

**If Your Cat Had Wet FIP:**

Watch for:
- Pot-bellied appearance returning (fluid in abdomen)
- Difficulty breathing (fluid in chest)
- Weight gain from fluid (not muscle)

**If Your Cat Had Neurological FIP:**

Watch for:
- Return of wobbling or balance issues
- Head tilt
- Seizures
- Behavior changes
- Weakness in limbs

**If Your Cat Had Ocular FIP:**

Watch for:
- Eye cloudiness or color changes
- Squinting or eye discomfort
- Vision problems (bumping into things)
- Redness or inflammation

**If Your Cat Had Dry FIP:**

Watch for:
- Signs depend on which organs were affected
- General malaise
- Decreased appetite
- Weight loss
- May be subtle initially

## What to Do If You Suspect Relapse

### Step 1: Don't Panic

**Remember:**
- Early detection is good (easier to treat)
- Most relapses respond well to retreatment
- Many "suspected relapses" turn out to be something else

### Step 2: Document What You're Seeing

**Keep a Log:**
- What symptoms are you noticing?
- When did they start?
- How severe are they?
- Any changes in weight, appetite, energy?
- Any fever?

**Take Photos/Videos:**
- If visible changes (pot-bellied, eye changes, etc.)
- If neurological signs (wobbling)
- Helps veterinarian assess

### Step 3: Contact Your Veterinarian Promptly

**Call Your Vet If:**
- Any combination of decreased appetite + lethargy + weight loss
- Fever lasting >24 hours
- Return of pot-bellied appearance
- Neurological signs
- Eye changes
- Any signs specific to your cat's original FIP form

**What to Communicate:**
- "[Cat's name] completed FIP treatment [X] months ago"
- "I'm seeing [specific symptoms]"
- "Started [date]"
- "Here's what I've documented..."

### Step 4: Veterinary Evaluation

**Your Vet Will Likely:**

✓ **Physical Examination**
- Check weight
- Assess body condition
- Palpate abdomen (fluid?)
- Listen to chest
- Check eyes
- Neurological assessment if indicated

✓ **Bloodwork**
- CBC (anemia, lymphopenia?)
- Chemistry panel
- **Most Important:** Albumin, globulin, A:G ratio
  - If A:G ratio decreasing or globulins increasing → concerning for relapse

✓ **Imaging (if indicated)**
- Abdominal ultrasound (look for effusion)
- Chest X-rays or ultrasound (pleural effusion?)

✓ **Effusion Analysis (if present)**
- Rivalta test
- Protein content
- PCR

### Step 5: Diagnosis and Treatment

**If Confirmed Relapse:**

**Treatment Protocol:**
- **Restart FIP treatment**
- Usually same medication (GS-441524)
- **May use higher dose** than original treatment
- **Longer duration:** 16-24 weeks instead of 12-16 weeks
- **Careful monitoring**

**Success Rate for Retreatment:**
- **80-85% of relapses respond well to retreatment**
- Similar protocol to original treatment
- Some cats may need extended or even indefinite maintenance therapy

**If NOT Relapse:**
- Your cat may have a different condition
- Treat accordingly
- Continue FIP monitoring schedule

## Living with a "FIP Survivor"

### Your Cat Can Live a Normal Life

**Good News:**
- Cats who complete treatment and don't relapse can live normal, full lifespans
- They can play, interact, and do everything other cats do
- FIP survivors often thrive!

### Lifestyle After FIP

**Multi-Cat Households:**
- ✅ Your FIP survivor can live with other cats
- ✅ FIP itself is not contagious
- ✅ Other cats are not at risk

**Normal Activities:**
- ✅ Play, exercise, normal activity
- ✅ Go outdoors (if previously outdoor cat) - though indoor is safer
- ✅ Interact with other cats and people
- ✅ Travel (with appropriate planning)

**Vaccinations:**
- ✅ Resume normal vaccination schedule
- ✅ Wait at least 4 weeks after stopping FIP treatment
- ✅ DO NOT use FIP vaccine (intranasal FCoV vaccine) - not recommended

**Spay/Neuter:**
- ✅ Safe to spay/neuter after FIP treatment completed
- ✅ Wait at least 4 weeks post-treatment
- ✅ Ensure bloodwork normal before anesthesia

### Minimizing Stress

**Why It Matters:**
- Stress can affect immune function
- Keep FIP survivors as stress-free as possible

**Strategies:**
- Maintain consistent routine
- Avoid unnecessary changes (moving, new pets, etc.) in first 6 months post-treatment
- Provide enrichment (play, toys, cat trees)
- Multiple litter boxes if multi-cat household
- Feliway or calming aids if anxious cat

### Nutrition

**Feed High-Quality Diet:**
- Support immune health
- Maintain healthy weight
- Kitten food if young cat (higher calories for growth)

**Monitor Weight:**
- Obesity is not healthy
- Underweight suggests possible problem
- Maintain ideal body condition

## FAQs About Post-Treatment Monitoring

### How long do I need to watch for relapse?

**Most relapses occur within 3-6 months.** After 6 months, relapse risk is very low. After 12 months, your cat is considered cured and unlikely to relapse.

**Recommended monitoring:**
- Intensive: First 3 months
- Moderate: Months 4-6
- Routine: After 6 months

### What if my cat has a minor illness (URI, GI upset)?

**Normal illnesses can still happen!**

- Minor upper respiratory infections (URI)
- Vomiting/diarrhea (dietary indiscretion, hairballs)
- Other common cat ailments

**These are NOT FIP relapse** unless accompanied by FIP-specific signs.

**Action:**
- Treat the illness as you normally would
- Monitor closely
- If not improving quickly or FIP signs develop, contact vet

### Can I stop worrying after treatment?

**Yes, eventually!**

- First 3 months: Stay vigilant but don't obsess
- Months 4-6: Relax a bit, continue monitoring
- After 6-12 months: Return to normal cat ownership mindset

**Balance:**
- Be aware, not paranoid
- Monitor systematically
- Enjoy your healthy cat!

### Should I test for coronavirus?

**No, routine coronavirus testing is NOT helpful:**

- ❌ Fecal PCR: Meaningless (many cats positive without FIP)
- ❌ Antibody tests: Not diagnostic for relapse
- ❌ Blood PCR: Not reliable

**Only useful tests for relapse:**
- ✓ Bloodwork (A:G ratio, globulins)
- ✓ Effusion analysis if fluid present
- ✓ Clinical signs + physical exam

### What if I need to travel?

**You can travel after FIP treatment!**

**Considerations:**
- Wait at least 1-2 months post-treatment
- Ensure cat is stable
- Arrange for reliable pet care
- Leave veterinary contact information
- Consider travel insurance for your cat

**Pet Sitters:**
- Brief them on FIP history
- Provide monitoring instructions
- Leave emergency vet contact

### Will my cat need medication forever?

**Most cats do NOT need lifelong medication.**

- 90-95% are cured with one treatment course
- 5-10% relapse and need retreatment
- Very few (

<2%) need extended/indefinite maintenance therapy

**Long-term medication rare, only if:**
- Multiple relapses
- Cannot achieve complete remission
- Veterinarian recommends based on individual case

## When to Resume "Normal" Cat Life

### Month 3 Post-Treatment

**If all normal at Month 3 recheck:**
- Reduce home monitoring frequency (biweekly weights)
- Feel more confident
- Plan that travel, major changes (if needed)

### Month 6 Post-Treatment

**If all normal at Month 6 recheck:**
- Your cat is very unlikely to relapse
- Resume normal annual vet visits
- Treat like any healthy cat
- Enjoy your FIP survivor!

### Month 12 Post-Treatment

**Congratulations - your cat is cured!**
- Relapse extremely unlikely after 12 months
- Return to routine annual wellness exams
- FIP is in the past

## Red Flags Checklist

**Contact your veterinarian promptly if you notice:**

- [ ] Weight loss >5% in 2 weeks
- [ ] Decreased appetite lasting >2-3 days
- [ ] Lethargy or hiding for multiple days
- [ ] Fever (>102.5°F/39°C) lasting >24 hours
- [ ] Pot-bellied appearance (fluid accumulation)
- [ ] Difficulty breathing
- [ ] Return of neurological signs (if had neurological FIP)
- [ ] Eye cloudiness or changes (if had ocular FIP)
- [ ] Any combination of subtle symptoms that concern you

**Trust your instincts.** You know your cat best. If something seems wrong, call your vet.

## Summary

**Most Important Points:**

1. **Relapse happens in only 5-10% of cats** - most never relapse
2. **Watch closely for 3-6 months post-treatment** - highest risk period
3. **Know the warning signs** - decreased appetite, lethargy, weight loss
4. **Keep vet appointments** - regular bloodwork is key
5. **Contact vet promptly if concerned** - early detection helps
6. **Most relapses respond to retreatment** - not a catastrophe
7. **After 6-12 months, your cat is cured** - relax and enjoy your healthy cat!

**Your cat beat FIP. That's amazing. With careful monitoring and a bit of vigilance, your cat can enjoy a long, healthy, happy life.**
