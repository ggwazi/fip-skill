# Combination and Adjunctive Therapy for FIP

> **Purpose:** Evidence-based guidance on combination antiviral protocols and supportive treatments
> **Audience:** Veterinarians managing FIP cases
> **Evidence Base:** UC Davis research, published case series, ABCD Guidelines 2024
> **Important:** Much of this information is based on case reports and theoretical rationale. Clearly distinguish established from experimental approaches.

---

## Overview

While GS-441524 monotherapy achieves cure rates of 70-85% in most FIP cases, combination and adjunctive therapies may be beneficial in:
- Refractory cases not responding to monotherapy
- Severe presentations requiring additional support
- Neurological or ocular involvement
- Cases with significant inflammatory component

**This document reviews:**
- Combination antiviral strategies
- Immunomodulatory therapies
- Anti-inflammatory approaches
- Supportive care protocols
- Nutritional interventions
- Pain management
- Drug interactions and contraindications

---

## Evidence Quality Framework

Throughout this document, therapies are rated by evidence quality:

- **⭐⭐⭐ Strong Evidence:** Controlled studies, large case series
- **⭐⭐ Moderate Evidence:** Small case series, consistent anecdotal reports
- **⭐ Limited Evidence:** Individual case reports, theoretical rationale
- **❓ Experimental:** No published clinical evidence, theoretical only

## Combination Antiviral Therapies

### GS-441524 + Remdesivir

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐ Limited Evidence

**Rationale:**
- Both are nucleoside analogs targeting viral RNA polymerase
- Different pharmacokinetics may provide complementary benefits
- Remdesivir is GS-441524's prodrug with potentially better CNS penetration
- Theoretical synergistic effect

**Published Experience:**
- Limited case reports
- Primarily used in refractory neurological cases
- No controlled studies comparing to monotherapy

**Protocol (based on case reports):**

**GS-441524 Component:**
- Continue at current effective dose (10-15 mg/kg for neurological)
- Subcutaneous or oral route

**Remdesivir Component:**
- **Initial IV loading:** Variable protocols reported
  - 5-10 mg/kg IV once, then maintenance
  - Or 2-5 mg/kg IV q24h for 3-5 days
- **Maintenance (if transition to subcutaneous):**
  - Protocols vary widely in literature
  - No standardized dosing
  - Availability and cost are significant barriers

**Challenges:**
- **Availability:** Remdesivir difficult to obtain for veterinary use
- **Cost:** Extremely expensive
- **Administration:** IV access required for loading
- **Evidence:** Primarily anecdotal
- **No clear data showing superiority to optimized GS-441524 monotherapy**

**Recommendation:**
- Consider only in refractory neurological cases after failed dose escalation
- Discuss experimental nature with owners
- Document outcomes for medical literature
- Most cases do well with appropriately dosed GS-441524 alone

### GS-441524 + Molnupiravir

**Evidence Quality:** ❓ Experimental

**Rationale:**
- Molnupiravir is another nucleoside analog antiviral
- Approved for human COVID-19
- Theoretical activity against FIP virus
- Oral bioavailability

**Current Status:**
- No published veterinary use for FIP
- Theoretical only
- Not recommended at this time

### Switching Between Formulations

**GS-441524 Oral ⟷ Injectable**

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐⭐ Moderate Evidence

**When to Switch:**

**Oral → Injectable:**
- Inadequate response to oral therapy
- Vomiting/diarrhea affecting absorption
- Neurological disease (injectable may have better bioavailability)
- Compliance with oral dosing difficult

**Injectable → Oral:**
- Severe injection site reactions
- Granuloma formation limiting injection sites
- Owner unable to give injections
- Cat extremely reactive to injections

**Considerations:**
- Oral bioavailability is lower than injectable
- May need higher oral dose to match injectable efficacy
- Injectable more predictable for severe cases
- Switching mid-treatment is acceptable if medically indicated

## Immunomodulatory Therapies

### Polyprenyl Immunostimulant (PI)

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐⭐ Moderate Evidence

**Mechanism:**
- Immune system modulator
- Enhances cellular immunity
- May reduce viral replication indirectly

**Published Evidence:**
- Some case reports showing benefit
- Theoretical immune support during FIP treatment
- Pre-2019 studies showed minimal effect as monotherapy
- No controlled studies combined with GS-441524

**Protocol:**
- **Dose:** Follow manufacturer guidelines
- **Route:** Oral
- **Duration:** Throughout FIP treatment and potentially beyond
- **Cost:** Moderate

**Potential Benefits:**
- May support immune clearance of virus
- Well-tolerated, minimal side effects
- Could theoretically reduce relapse risk

**Limitations:**
- No clear evidence of added benefit with GS-441524
- Cost adds to overall treatment expense
- Effect difficult to isolate from GS-441524 efficacy

**Recommendation:**
- Optional adjunct
- More data needed to establish clear benefit
- Reasonable to try in refractory cases
- Not essential for standard cases

### Feline Interferon Omega

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐ Limited Evidence

**Availability:** Licensed in Europe and Japan; not approved in USA

**Mechanism:**
- Antiviral and immunomodulatory effects
- Anti-inflammatory properties
- Enhances immune response

**Protocol (where available):**
- **Dose:** 1 million units/kg SC q24h initially, taper to 3x weekly
- **Duration:** Variable, often throughout treatment
- **Cost:** Expensive

**Evidence:**
- Pre-GS-441524 era: minimal efficacy as monotherapy
- Limited reports combined with GS-441524
- No controlled studies showing added benefit

**Recommendation:**
- May be considered where available
- No strong evidence supporting routine use with GS-441524
- Cost-benefit ratio unclear

### LTCI (Lymphocyte T-Cell Immunomodulator)

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐ Limited Evidence

**Status:**
- Availability variable
- Limited published data
- Primarily anecdotal reports

**Recommendation:**
- Insufficient evidence to recommend routinely
- May be considered in refractory cases
- More research needed

## Anti-Inflammatory Therapy

### Corticosteroids

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐⭐ Moderate Evidence (conflicting)

**The Controversy:**

**Arguments FOR Use:**
- FIP pathology is largely inflammatory
- Reducing inflammation may limit organ damage
- Can improve appetite and quality of life
- Some practitioners report better outcomes with concurrent steroids

**Arguments AGAINST Use:**
- Immunosuppression may impair viral clearance
- Historical failure of steroids as monotherapy
- May mask progression of disease
- Theoretical interference with antiviral effectiveness

**Current Consensus:**
- **No clear evidence supporting routine use**
- **May have role in specific situations**
- **Use lowest effective dose if employed**
- **Taper as soon as clinically able**

**Indications for Consideration:**

1. **Severe Inflammatory Component:**
   - Large effusions causing respiratory distress
   - Severe ocular inflammation
   - Marked neurological inflammation (with higher GS-441524 doses)

2. **Appetite Stimulation:**
   - Cats not eating despite GS-441524
   - Severe weight loss
   - Short-term use to improve quality of life

3. **Inflammatory Crisis:**
   - Worsening inflammation despite antiviral therapy
   - Suspected immune-mediated component

**Protocol (if using):**

**Prednisolone:**
- **Initial dose:** 0.5-1 mg/kg PO q12-24h
- **Duration:** Short-term only (days to weeks, not months)
- **Taper:** Reduce gradually as clinical signs improve
- **Goal:** Discontinue within 2-4 weeks if possible

**Monitoring:**
- Watch for masking of disease progression
- Monitor appetite, energy (may artificially improve)
- Continue assessing objective parameters (bloodwork, imaging)

**Contraindications:**
- FeLV/FIV co-infection (relative contraindication)
- Concurrent bacterial infection
- Diabetes mellitus
- Severe immunosuppression

**Recommendation:**
- **Not routine first-line therapy**
- Consider in specific situations listed above
- Use judiciously with clear indication
- Taper and discontinue as soon as possible
- Document outcomes to add to evidence base

### NSAIDs

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐ Limited Evidence

**General Recommendation:** **Avoid in FIP cases**

**Reasons for Caution:**
- Risk of GI ulceration
- Potential renal compromise (FIP can affect kidneys)
- Dehydration risk
- Not clearly beneficial for FIP-related inflammation

**Rare Indications:**
- Severe pain uncontrolled by other methods
- Short-term use only
- Ensure adequate hydration
- Monitor renal function closely

**If Using NSAIDs:**
- Meloxicam: 0.05 mg/kg PO q24h (conservative dose)
- Robenacoxib: 1 mg/kg PO q24h
- Use for shortest duration possible
- Avoid in cats with renal compromise or dehydration

## Supportive Care Protocols

### Appetite Stimulants

**Evidence Quality:** ⭐⭐⭐ Strong Evidence (for efficacy of drugs, not FIP-specific)

**Mirtazapine:**
- **Dose:** 1.88-3.75 mg per cat PO q48-72h
- **Mechanism:** Antiemetic, appetite stimulant, mood elevator
- **Benefits:** Well-tolerated, effective, addresses nausea
- **Caution:** Adjust dose in renal disease
- **Evidence:** Strong for appetite stimulation; improves quality of life

**Maropitant (Cerenia):**
- **Dose:** 1-2 mg/kg PO/SC q24h
- **Mechanism:** Antiemetic
- **Benefits:** Reduces nausea, may have anti-inflammatory effects
- **Evidence:** Excellent antiemetic; indirect appetite improvement

**Capromorelin:**
- **Dose:** 2 mg/kg PO q24h
- **Mechanism:** Ghrelin receptor agonist
- **Benefits:** Effective appetite stimulation
- **Cost:** Expensive
- **Evidence:** Strong for appetite stimulation

**Cyproheptadine:**
- **Dose:** 2-4 mg per cat PO q12-24h
- **Mechanism:** Antihistamine with appetite-stimulating effects
- **Benefits:** Inexpensive, well-tolerated
- **Evidence:** Moderate; less effective than mirtazapine in most cats

**Recommendation:**
- Start with mirtazapine + maropitant combination (addresses appetite and nausea)
- Add capromorelin if inadequate response and financially feasible
- Use feeding tubes (esophagostomy) if medications insufficient

### Nutritional Support

**High-Calorie Diets:**
- **Critical for recovery**
- Provide calorie-dense foods
- Offer multiple small meals throughout day
- Warm food to enhance palatability
- Try highly palatable options (baby food, tuna water, etc.)

**Feeding Tube Placement:**

**Indications:**
- Not eating despite appetite stimulants
- Continued weight loss
- Unable to meet caloric needs voluntarily

**Type:**
- **Esophagostomy tube:** Preferred for FIP (long-term, easy to use)
- **Nasogastric:** Short-term only, less ideal
- **Gastrostomy/PEG:** Requires general anesthesia; higher risk

**Benefits:**
- Ensures adequate nutrition during treatment
- Reduces stress of force-feeding
- Allows precise caloric intake
- Can administer oral medications

**Omega-3 Fatty Acids:**
- **Dose:** Fish oil providing EPA/DHA
- **Benefits:** Anti-inflammatory, may support immune function
- **Evidence:** Theoretical benefit; well-tolerated
- **Recommendation:** Reasonable adjunct, low risk

### Fluid Therapy

**Indications:**
- Dehydration (assess skin turgor, mucous membranes)
- Not drinking adequately
- Vomiting/diarrhea
- Azotemia on bloodwork

**Subcutaneous Fluids:**
- **Dose:** 50-100 mL per cat q12-24h as needed
- **Fluid:** LRS or Normosol-R preferred
- **Duration:** Until eating/drinking adequately
- **Owner administration:** Can be taught for home use

**IV Fluids:**
- **Indication:** Severe dehydration, not tolerating SC fluids
- **Requires hospitalization**
- **Monitor for fluid overload (especially in cats with effusions)**

**Caution with Wet FIP:**
- Avoid over-aggressive fluid therapy
- Can worsen effusions
- Use judiciously based on hydration status

## Pain Management

### Assessment of Pain in FIP

**Signs of Pain:**
- Hunched posture
- Reluctance to move
- Hiding
- Vocalizing
- Decreased appetite
- Aggression when touched (abdomen, joints, etc.)

**Common Pain Sources in FIP:**
- Abdominal pain (peritonitis, effusion)
- Ocular pain (uveitis)
- Neurological pain (meningitis)
- Joint/muscle pain
- Injection site pain

### Pain Medications

**Buprenorphine:**
- **Dose:** 0.01-0.03 mg/kg PO/SL/SC/IV q6-12h
- **Benefits:** Effective, well-tolerated, multiple routes
- **Evidence:** Strong for pain relief
- **Recommendation:** First-line for moderate to severe pain

**Gabapentin:**
- **Dose:** 5-10 mg/kg PO q8-12h
- **Benefits:** Neuropathic pain, anxiety reduction, sedation
- **Evidence:** Good for neurological and chronic pain
- **Recommendation:** Excellent for neurological FIP; consider in all painful cases

**Tramadol:**
- **Dose:** 2-4 mg/kg PO q12h
- **Benefits:** Moderate pain relief
- **Evidence:** Variable in cats; less predictable than buprenorphine
- **Recommendation:** Second-line option

**Fentanyl Patches:**
- **Dose:** Variable by patch size
- **Benefits:** Long-acting (72 hours)
- **Limitations:** Expensive, variable absorption in thin cats
- **Recommendation:** Consider for severe pain when frequent dosing difficult

**Local/Regional Pain Management:**
- **Injection site pain:** Ice packs, warm compresses, rotation
- **Abdominal pain:** Gentle heat application
- **Ocular pain:** Topical atropine for uveitis (dilates pupil, reduces ciliary spasm)

## Specific Organ Support

### Hepatic Support

**Indications:**
- Elevated liver enzymes
- Icterus
- Hepatic lipidosis risk (not eating)

**S-Adenosylmethionine (SAMe):**
- **Dose:** 20 mg/kg PO q24h on empty stomach
- **Benefits:** Antioxidant, hepatoprotectant
- **Evidence:** Moderate for hepatic support

**Ursodiol:**
- **Dose:** 10-15 mg/kg PO q24h
- **Benefits:** Choleretic, anti-inflammatory
- **Evidence:** Good for cholestatic conditions

### Renal Support

**Indications:**
- Elevated BUN/creatinine
- Dry FIP affecting kidneys
- Dehydration

**Strategies:**
- Fluid therapy (SC or IV)
- Phosphate binders if hyperphosphatemic
- Renal diet if eating
- Monitor closely during treatment

### GI Support

**Probiotics:**
- Consider if diarrhea present
- Evidence: Limited but low risk

**Famotidine/Omeprazole:**
- If GI signs suggest ulceration
- Dose: Famotidine 0.5-1 mg/kg q12-24h; Omeprazole 0.7-1 mg/kg q24h

## Effusion Management

### Drainage Procedures

**Indications for Therapeutic Drainage:**
- Respiratory distress (thoracic effusion)
- Severe abdominal distension causing discomfort
- Diagnostic sampling needed

**Abdominocentesis:**
- **Technique:** Ultrasound-guided preferred
- **Volume:** Remove as much as needed for comfort
- **Frequency:** As needed for symptomatic relief
- **Note:** Effusion typically resolves with GS-441524; repeated drainage needed less as treatment progresses

**Thoracocentesis:**
- **Indication:** Dyspnea from pleural effusion
- **Technique:** Standard pleural tap
- **Monitoring:** Respiratory rate and effort post-procedure

**Expected Timeline:**
- Effusion production slows within 1-2 weeks of effective treatment
- Complete resolution typically by 4-8 weeks
- Need for drainage should decrease rapidly if treatment working

## Drug Interactions and Contraindications

### GS-441524 Drug Interactions

**Minimal Known Interactions:**
- GS-441524 appears to have few drug interactions
- Can be used with most common medications

**Theoretical Concerns:**
- **Other antivirals:** Limited data on combinations
- **Immunosuppressants:** May reduce efficacy (corticosteroids at high doses)
- **Nephrotoxic drugs:** Use caution (GS-441524 renally excreted)

**Generally Safe Concurrent Use:**
- Antibiotics
- Anti-nausea medications
- Pain medications
- Appetite stimulants
- Cardiac medications
- Thyroid medications
- Insulin (for diabetic cats)

### Medications to Avoid or Use Cautiously

**NSAIDs:**
- Risk of renal compromise
- GI ulceration risk
- Use only if necessary with close monitoring

**High-Dose Corticosteroids:**
- May suppress immune viral clearance
- Use lowest dose for shortest duration

**Chemotherapy:**
- Immunosuppression contraindicated
- Exception: Lymphoma misdiagnosed as FIP requires reconsideration

**Nephrotoxic Drugs:**
- Aminoglycosides
- Amphotericin
- Use alternatives if possible

## Treatment of Concurrent Conditions

### Bacterial Infections

**Common in FIP:**
- Secondary to immunosuppression
- Can complicate diagnosis and treatment

**Approach:**
- Culture effusions if suspected
- Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics if indicated
  - Amoxicillin-clavulanate: 12.5-22 mg/kg PO q12h
  - Enrofloxacin: 5 mg/kg PO q24h (avoid in young kittens)
- Re-evaluate need as FIP treatment progresses

### Parasites

**Intestinal Parasites:**
- Common in young cats with FIP
- Treat appropriately
- Fecal exam recommended

**Toxoplasmosis:**
- Can mimic neurological FIP
- Test if neurological signs present
- Treat with clindamycin if positive

### Chronic Diseases

**Diabetes Mellitus:**
- Can complicate FIP treatment (corticosteroid use challenging)
- Insulin therapy continues
- Monitor glucose closely

**Hyperthyroidism:**
- Continue thyroid medication
- Monitor as appetite changes may affect dosing

**Chronic Kidney Disease:**
- Careful fluid management
- Monitor renal function closely during GS-441524 treatment
- May affect prognosis

## Monitoring Combined Therapies

### Comprehensive Monitoring Protocol

**Weekly:**
- Weight
- Temperature
- Clinical assessment
- Appetite/energy/attitude log

**Every 2-4 Weeks:**
- CBC, chemistry panel
- Albumin:Globulin ratio tracking
- Abdominal ultrasound if wet FIP

**Monitor for Adverse Effects:**

**GS-441524:**
- Injection site reactions
- Elevated liver enzymes (monitor if using hepatotoxic drugs concurrently)

**Corticosteroids (if using):**
- Polydipsia/polyuria
- Polyphagia
- Behavioral changes
- Immunosuppression signs

**Pain Medications:**
- Sedation
- GI upset
- Behavioral changes

**Appetite Stimulants:**
- Hypersalivation (mirtazapine if given too frequently)
- Behavioral changes

## Cost Considerations

### Combination Therapy Costs

**Adding therapies increases financial burden:**

**Relative Costs:**
- **GS-441524:** Variable by source (largest expense)
- **Corticosteroids:** Inexpensive ($10-30/month)
- **Appetite stimulants:** Moderate ($30-100/month)
- **Pain medications:** Moderate ($20-80/month)
- **Polyprenyl Immunostimulant:** Moderate to expensive
- **Remdesivir:** Extremely expensive (often prohibitive)

**Cost-Benefit Discussion:**
- Prioritize therapies most likely to help
- GS-441524 is the foundation; everything else is supportive
- Target adjuncts to specific symptoms/problems
- Avoid "kitchen sink" approach

## Evidence-Based Recommendations Summary

### **Established (Use Routinely):**

**⭐⭐⭐ Strong Support:**
- **GS-441524 monotherapy** (foundation of treatment)
- **Appetite stimulants** (mirtazapine, maropitant)
- **Pain management** (buprenorphine, gabapentin)
- **Nutritional support** (high-calorie diet, feeding tubes if needed)
- **Fluid therapy** (for dehydration)

### **Reasonable to Consider:**

**⭐⭐ Moderate Support:**
- **Switching formulations** (oral ⟷ injectable for specific indications)
- **Hepatic support** (SAMe, ursodiol if elevated liver enzymes)
- **Omega-3 fatty acids** (low risk, potential benefit)

### **Unclear Benefit, May Try in Refractory Cases:**

**⭐ Limited Evidence:**
- **Short-term corticosteroids** (specific indications, lowest dose, taper quickly)
- **Polyprenyl Immunostimulant** (theoretical benefit, no clear evidence)
- **Feline interferon** (where available; limited evidence)

### **Experimental, Not Recommended Routinely:**

**❓ Insufficient Evidence:**
- **GS-441524 + Remdesivir** (expensive, limited evidence, availability issues)
- **Other combination antivirals** (no clinical data)
- **Routine NSAIDs** (risks outweigh benefits in most cases)

## Clinical Decision Algorithm

### Uncomplicated FIP Case

**Foundation:**
1. GS-441524 at appropriate dose
2. Appetite stimulant if not eating well
3. Nutritional support
4. Monitoring protocol

**Add as Needed:**
- Pain management (if signs of pain)
- Fluid therapy (if dehydrated)

### Refractory Case (Not Responding to Standard Treatment)

**Intensify Foundation:**
1. Increase GS-441524 dose by 25-50%
2. Switch formulation if on oral
3. Ensure compliance and medication quality

**Consider Adding:**
- Short-term corticosteroids (if severe inflammation)
- Polyprenyl Immunostimulant
- Aggressive supportive care

**Reevaluate:**
- Reconsider diagnosis
- Assess for concurrent disease
- Discuss prognosis with owner

### Neurological FIP

**Foundation:**
1. GS-441524 10-15 mg/kg
2. Gabapentin for neuropathic pain
3. Nutritional support

**Consider:**
- GS-441524 + Remdesivir (if available, severe cases)
- Short-term corticosteroids (if severe CNS inflammation)

### Severe Presentation

**Stabilization:**
1. Address life-threatening issues (respiratory distress, seizures)
2. Drain effusions if needed
3. IV fluids if severely dehydrated

**Foundation Treatment:**
1. GS-441524 at appropriate dose
2. Pain management
3. Nutritional support
4. Close monitoring

**Adjuncts:**
- Consider corticosteroids for severe inflammation
- Antiemetics
- Appetite stimulants

## Future Directions

### Areas Needing Research

**Unanswered Questions:**
1. Does combination antiviral therapy improve outcomes over optimized GS-441524 monotherapy?
2. What role, if any, do corticosteroids play in FIP treatment outcomes?
3. Can immunomodulators reduce relapse rates?
4. What is optimal dosing and duration to minimize relapse?

**Ongoing:**
- Case reports continue to emerge

## Summary

**Foundation of FIP Treatment:**
- **GS-441524 is the cornerstone** - everything else is supportive

**Established Adjuncts:**
- Appetite stimulation
- Pain management
- Nutritional support
- Symptom-directed supportive care

**Experimental Approaches:**
- Most combination therapies lack strong evidence
- May be reasonable in refractory cases
- Document outcomes to advance knowledge

**Key Principle:**
- Use evidence-based adjuncts to support the cat during GS-441524 treatment
- Avoid unproven therapies adding cost without clear benefit
- Focus on quality of life and symptom management
