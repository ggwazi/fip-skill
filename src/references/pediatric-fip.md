# Pediatric FIP: Special Considerations for Kittens

> **Purpose:** Evidence-based guidance for managing FIP in kittens, especially those <12 weeks old
> **Audience:** Veterinarians treating young kittens with FIP
> **Evidence Base:** UC Davis research, pediatric case series, ABCD Guidelines 2024
> **Note:** Limited published data specific to neonatal/pediatric FIP; recommendations based on case reports and clinical experience

---

## Overview

FIP in kittens, particularly those under 12 weeks of age, presents unique diagnostic and therapeutic challenges. While young cats (6-24 months) are the most common age group for FIP, **neonatal and early pediatric FIP** (birth to 12 weeks) requires special consideration.

**Special challenges in pediatric FIP:**
- Small body size complicates dosing and administration
- Rapid growth requires frequent dose adjustments
- Maternal antibodies interfere with diagnostic testing
- More fragile patients with less physiologic reserve
- Litter management and breeder considerations
- Ethical and prognostic questions unique to neonates

---

## Age Classifications and Considerations

### Neonatal FIP (Birth to 4 Weeks)

**Extremely Rare but Devastating:**
- Suggests in utero or periparturient infection
- Often affects multiple littermates
- Very poor prognosis due to underdeveloped immune system
- Treatment extremely challenging

**Presentation:**
- Failure to thrive
- "Fading kitten syndrome"
- Neurological signs
- Rarely effusive at this age

**Challenges:**
- Tiny size (<500g often)
- Cannot regulate body temperature
- Nursing dependent
- Injection volumes problematic
- Diagnostic testing difficult (small blood volumes)

**Prognosis:**
- Very guarded (<50% success even with treatment)
- Many too small/fragile to survive treatment
- Ethical considerations significant

### Early Pediatric FIP (4-12 Weeks)

**More Common, Challenging but Treatable:**
- Often seen in shelter/cattery kittens
- Weaned or weaning
- Growing rapidly
- More resilient than neonates

**Typical Weight Range:** 500g-1500g

**Presentation:**
- Wet FIP most common
- "Pot-bellied" appearance
- Failure to gain weight appropriately
- Lethargy, poor appetite

**Challenges:**
- Rapid growth requires weekly dose recalculation
- Small injection volumes challenging
- Limited subcutaneous space
- Diagnostic testing complicated by maternal antibodies

**Prognosis:**
- Good to excellent with appropriate treatment (70-85%)
- Better than neonates, similar to older kittens
- Key is precise dosing and careful monitoring

### Juvenile FIP (12 Weeks to 6 Months)

**Most Common Pediatric Group:**
- Fully weaned
- Growing but more stable weight
- Post-vaccination age

**Challenges:**
- Still growing (dose adjustments needed)
- Vaccines may complicate diagnosis
- Often from shelters/catteries with high FCoV exposure

**Prognosis:**
- Excellent (80-90% success rate)
- Similar to older cats
- Fewer technical challenges than younger kittens

## Diagnostic Considerations in Pediatric FIP

### Maternal Antibody Interference

**The Problem:**
- Kittens receive FCoV antibodies through colostrum
- Maternal antibodies persist up to 10-14 weeks
- Antibody tests cannot distinguish maternal from kitten's own antibodies
- **Positive FCoV antibody test is meaningless in kittens <14 weeks**

**Implications:**
- Cannot use coronavirus antibody testing for diagnosis
- Must rely on other diagnostic criteria
- PCR on effusion is most useful if wet FIP

### Diagnostic Approach in Kittens

**Wet FIP in Kittens:**

**Highly Supportive:**
- ✅ Effusion with high protein (>3.5 g/dL)
- ✅ Positive Rivalta test
- ✅ Positive RT-PCR on effusion (high specificity)
- ✅ High globulin, low A:G ratio
- ✅ Lymphopenia, non-regenerative anemia

**Less Useful:**
- ❌ Coronavirus antibody testing (maternal antibodies)
- ❌ Coronavirus PCR on feces (many kittens positive without FIP)

**Dry FIP in Kittens:**
- More challenging diagnosis
- Rely on clinical signs, imaging, bloodwork
- Consider presumptive diagnosis and therapeutic trial

### Differential Diagnoses in Kittens

**Rule Out:**
- **Bacterial peritonitis** (septic abdomen)
- **Toxoplasmosis** (neurological signs)
- **Parasites** (ascites from severe infestation)
- **Congenital defects** (heart failure, liver shunts)
- **Trauma** (abdomen, CNS)
- **Panleukopenia** (FPV) - can cause effusion in severe cases
- **Sepsis** from umbilical infection (neonates)

## Treatment Protocols for Pediatric FIP

### Dosing Calculations

**Standard Dosing Applies:**
- **Wet/dry FIP:** 4-6 mg/kg
- **Neurological FIP:** 10-12 mg/kg
- **Ocular FIP:** 8-10 mg/kg

**Critical Differences:**
1. **Weekly Weight Checks Mandatory**
   - Kittens double weight in weeks
   - Failing to increase dose = underdosing
   - **Weigh every 3-7 days** and recalculate

2. **Accurate Weight Measurement**
   - Use gram scale for kittens <1 kg
   - Digital kitchen scale acceptable if accurate
   - Weigh at same time of day

3. **Precise Dose Calculation**
   - Small kittens: calculate to 0.01 mL precision
   - Errors in tiny volumes = significant dose errors

**Example Calculation:**

Kitten: 600g (0.6 kg), wet FIP
- Dose: 6 mg/kg
- Total dose: 0.6 kg × 6 mg/kg = 3.6 mg
- If concentration is 15 mg/mL:
  - Volume = 3.6 mg ÷ 15 mg/mL = 0.24 mL
- **Must measure 0.24 mL accurately**

### Injection Technique for Small Kittens

**Challenges:**
- Limited subcutaneous space
- Small injection volumes (0.1-0.5 mL typical)
- Thin skin, easy to penetrate through
- Fragile tissues

**Best Practices:**

**Needle Selection:**
- **25-27 gauge** preferred (smaller causes more tissue damage, larger more painful)
- **5/8 inch or 1/2 inch** length
- Insulin syringes work well (has 0.01 mL markings)

**Injection Sites for Small Kittens:**
- **Scruff** (between shoulder blades)
- **Lateral thorax** (either side of chest wall)
- **Flank** (either side)
- **Rotate daily** - critical in small kittens (limited area)

**Technique:**
1. **Ensure SC, not IM or intradermal:**
   - Tent skin gently
   - Insert needle parallel to body wall
   - Aspirate slightly (no blood = correct placement)

2. **Small Volume Injection:**
   - Inject very slowly (10-15 seconds for 0.2 mL)
   - Prevents pressure damage to delicate tissues

3. **Post-Injection:**
   - Pinch skin closed for 5 seconds
   - Minimize leakage
   - Watch for swelling (should be small bump only)

**Site Rotation Critical:**
- With limited body surface area, site rotation essential
- Map injection sites on calendar
- Never same site two days in a row
- Ideally, 5-6 different sites in rotation

### Volume Limitations

**Maximum SC Volume:**
- Neonates (<500g): **0.5 mL maximum per site**
- Small kittens (500g-1kg): **0.5-1 mL per site**
- Larger kittens (1-2kg): **1-2 mL per site**

**If Dose Exceeds Maximum Volume:**

**Option 1: Split Dose**
- Divide into 2 injection sites
- Example: 0.8 mL total = 0.4 mL in two sites

**Option 2: Higher Concentration**
- If available, use more concentrated formulation
- Reduces volume needed

**Option 3: Twice Daily Dosing**
- Split total daily dose into q12h dosing
- Smaller volumes per injection
- More labor-intensive for owners

### Oral Administration in Kittens

**Considerations:**
- Easier for some owners than injections
- Lower bioavailability than injectable
- May be adequate for wet FIP (less so for neurological)

**Technique:**
- Oral syringe directly into mouth
- Ensure swallowed (not spit out)
- Give on empty stomach for better absorption

**Dosing:**
- May need 10-20% higher dose than injectable to compensate for lower bioavailability
- Monitor response closely

### Growth Monitoring During Treatment

**Track Both Weight and Body Condition:**

**Weekly Measurements:**
- **Weight (grams)**
- **Body condition score** (1-9 scale)
- **Length** (nose to tail base)
- **Girth** (chest circumference)

**Expected Growth in Healthy Kittens:**
- Gain approximately 50-100g per week
- Double birth weight by 10-14 days
- Triple by 3 weeks
- Quadruple by 4 weeks

**FIP Kittens on Treatment:**
- May not gain as rapidly initially (weeks 1-2)
- Should start gaining steadily by week 2-4
- Normal growth pattern resumes with successful treatment
- Track on growth curve

**Red Flags:**
- **Continued weight loss** after 2 weeks treatment
- **Failure to gain** after 4 weeks treatment
- **Poor body condition** despite treatment

**Dose Adjustments:**
- **Recalculate dose weekly** based on current weight
- Increase dose as kitten grows
- Failure to adjust = relative underdosing

## Nutritional Support in Pediatric FIP

### Caloric Needs of Growing Kittens

**Kittens have HIGH caloric requirements:**
- Growing requires 2-3× adult calories per kg
- FIP increases metabolic demands
- **Failure to eat = rapid decline**

**Daily Energy Requirements (DER):**
- **Kittens 4-12 weeks:** 200-250 kcal/kg/day
- **Older kittens:** 150-200 kcal/kg/day
- FIP may increase by 25-50%

### Feeding Strategies

**Nursing Kittens (<4 Weeks):**
- **Mother's milk ideal** if queen is healthy and kitten can nurse
- **Kitten milk replacer** if hand-raising
  - Feed every 2-4 hours
  - Follow product guidelines for volume
- **Monitor weight gain daily**

**Weaning Kittens (4-8 Weeks):**
- **Gruel/slurry** of kitten milk replacer + wet kitten food
- Offer small frequent meals (4-6x daily)
- Warm food to enhance palatability
- High-calorie kitten food

**Weaned Kittens (>8 Weeks):**
- High-calorie kitten food (wet preferred)
- Free-choice feeding if eating well
- 3-4 meals daily if appetite stimulation needed

**Appetite Stimulants in Kittens:**

**Mirtazapine:**
- **Dose:** 0.5-1 mg per kitten (very small kittens) q48-72h
- **Adjust carefully** - lower doses than adult cats
- **Monitor for sedation**

**Maropitant:**
- **Dose:** 1-2 mg/kg q24h
- **Can be used in kittens >8 weeks**
- **Antiemetic effect beneficial**

**Caution with Capromorelin:**
- Limited safety data in very young kittens
- Consider for kittens >12 weeks

### Feeding Tubes in Kittens

**Indications:**
- Not eating despite stimulants
- Weight loss continuing
- Too weak to eat

**Esophagostomy Tube:**
- **Feasible in kittens >4 weeks, >500g**
- Requires general anesthesia (risk in sick kittens)
- Allows precise caloric intake
- Can give oral medications through tube

**Nasogastric Tube:**
- Short-term option
- Smaller tube diameter
- Can be placed with sedation only

**Technique Modifications:**
- Smaller diameter tubes for kittens
- Calculate precise caloric delivery
- Feed small frequent volumes

## Monitoring and Complications

### Monitoring Protocol for Pediatric FIP

**Daily (Owner):**
- Weight (first 2 weeks, then weekly)
- Appetite (eating well vs. poor)
- Energy level
- Urination/defecation
- Body temperature (if kitten <8 weeks)

**Weekly (Veterinary):**
- Physical examination
- Weight on clinic scale
- Abdominal palpation (effusion assessment)
- Body condition scoring

**Every 2-4 Weeks (Veterinary):**
- Bloodwork (CBC, chemistry)
  - Note: Reference ranges differ in kittens
- A:G ratio trending
- Imaging if indicated

### Complications More Common in Kittens

**Hypoglycemia:**
- Small kittens have limited glycogen stores
- FIP + not eating = hypoglycemia risk
- **Monitor for weakness, seizures**
- **Treat with oral/IV dextrose**

**Hypothermia:**
- Kittens <4 weeks cannot thermoregulate
- Provide external heat source
- Monitor temperature

**Dehydration:**
- Rapid onset in small kittens
- Assess skin turgor, mucous membranes
- SC fluids dosed carefully (10-20 mL per site maximum)

**Injection Site Reactions:**
- Limited body surface area
- Granulomas, abscesses more impactful
- Monitor sites closely
- Consider oral formulation if severe

**Stress:**
- Handling, injections stressful for tiny kittens
- Minimize handling to essentials
- Keep with mother if possible

## Prognosis in Pediatric Patients

### Success Rates by Age Group

**Neonates (0-4 Weeks):**
- **Success rate: 30-50%** (estimate based on limited cases)
- Many too fragile to survive
- Significant ethical considerations

**Early Pediatric (4-12 Weeks):**
- **Success rate: 70-85%**
- Similar to older kittens if treatment tolerated
- Key is precise dosing and nutrition

**Juvenile (12 Weeks-6 Months):**
- **Success rate: 80-90%**
- Excellent prognosis with treatment
- Similar to adult cats

### Factors Affecting Prognosis in Kittens

**Positive Prognostic Factors:**
- ✅ Weight >500g at diagnosis
- ✅ Still eating
- ✅ Wet FIP (vs. neurological)
- ✅ Single kitten affected (vs. litter outbreak)
- ✅ Owner compliance with protocol

**Negative Prognostic Factors:**
- ❌ Severe failure to thrive
- ❌ Weight <500g
- ❌ Neurological involvement
- ❌ FeLV positive
- ❌ Litter outbreak (multiple kittens affected)
- ❌ Concurrent infections (panleukopenia, upper respiratory, etc.)

## Litter and Cattery Management

### When Multiple Kittens Are Affected

**Litter Outbreak Scenario:**
- Multiple littermates develop FIP
- Suggests high FCoV viral load in environment
- Queen may be shedding high levels of FCoV

**Management:**

1. **Isolate Affected Kittens**
   - Treat individually
   - Prevent spread of other pathogens
   - Reduce stress on healthy littermates

2. **Monitor Unaffected Littermates Closely**
   - Weekly weights
   - Watch for signs of FIP
   - **Note:** Having littermate with FIP does NOT mean others will get it
   - FCoV exposure is common; FIP development is not

3. **Environmental Management**
   - Scoop litter boxes 2-3× daily
   - Disinfect with dilute bleach
   - Separate feeding areas
   - Reduce crowding/stress

4. **Queen Management**
   - Queen can stay with healthy kittens
   - FIP itself is not contagious
   - Manage FCoV shedding (litter hygiene)

### Cattery/Breeder Considerations

**When Cattery Has FIP Case:**

**Short-Term Actions:**
1. Treat affected kitten(s)
2. Isolate from other litters
3. Enhance biosecurity
4. Monitor closely

**Long-Term Considerations:**

**If Single Isolated Case:**
- FIP can happen even in well-managed catteries
- Single case is not necessarily a cattery problem
- Continue good practices

**If Multiple Cases or Recurring FIP:**
- **High FCoV burden in cattery likely**
- Need cattery-wide FCoV management:
  - Early weaning protocols (5-6 weeks)
  - Isolation of queens and litters
  - Aggressive litter box hygiene
  - FCoV testing and removal of high shedders
  - Limit breeding colony size
  - Quarantine new cats

**Queen Breeding Decisions:**
- Queen whose kitten had FIP can be bred again
- FIP is not directly heritable
- Genetic predisposition possible but complex
- Monitor subsequent litters closely

**Kitten Sale Considerations:**
- Ethical obligation to disclose FIP in littermate
- Many healthy littermates never develop FIP
- Some breeders offer health guarantees
- Transparent communication with buyers

## Client Communication for Pediatric FIP

### Discussing Prognosis

**Emphasize:**
- Kittens can respond very well to treatment
- Success rates similar to older cats if kitten >4-8 weeks
- Requires commitment to careful dosing and monitoring

**Challenges to Discuss:**
- Frequent dose adjustments (weekly weight checks)
- Injection technique more difficult
- Nutritional support critical
- Higher cost proportional to longer treatment if kitten is young at start

**Realistic Expectations:**
- Kitten will continue to grow during treatment
- Medication cost increases as kitten grows
- Treatment duration same as adult cats (12-16+ weeks)
- Kitten may be 2-3× heavier at end of treatment than beginning

### Financial Counseling

**Medication Cost Considerations:**
- Kitten may start at 0.5 kg, end treatment at 2-3 kg
- Medication cost increases proportionally
- **Example:**
  - 0.6 kg kitten: 3.6 mg/day
  - Same kitten at 2 kg: 12 mg/day
  - **3× increase in medication cost**

**Total Treatment Cost:**
- Account for growth in estimate
- Provide range, not fixed price
- Discuss payment plans

**Cost-Benefit:**
- Treating young kitten provides full lifespan (12-20 years)
- Same investment as older cat but more years gained
- Frame as investment in kitten's life

## Special Populations

### Shelter Kittens with FIP

**Challenges:**
- Often unknown history
- May have concurrent infections
- Parasites common
- Nutritional deficits
- Stress of shelter environment

**Approach:**
- Treat FIP as primary issue
- Address concurrent infections
- Deworm
- Nutritional support aggressive
- Foster care ideal during treatment

**Adoption Considerations:**
- Some shelters treat and adopt out
- Adopter must commit to completing treatment
- Clear communication essential

### Feral/Semi-Feral Kittens

**Treatment Feasibility:**
- Very young feral kittens can be socialized during treatment
- Capture, socialize, treat, adopt
- Daily handling during treatment aids socialization

**Challenges:**
- Trapping for monitoring appointments
- Compliance if truly feral (not semi-feral)

## Long-Term Outcomes

### Growth and Development Post-Treatment

**Successful Treatment:**
- Kittens resume normal growth
- Reach normal adult size
- No developmental delays
- Normal lifespan expected

**Possible Long-Term Effects:**
- Injection site scarring (usually minimal)
- If neurological FIP: some deficits may persist
  - Often adapt well
  - Quality of life usually excellent

### Vaccination After FIP Treatment

**Timing:**
- Complete treatment course first
- Wait at least 4 weeks after stopping FIP treatment
- Ensure bloodwork normalized

**Vaccine Considerations:**
- Safe to vaccinate after successful FIP treatment
- Follow standard kitten vaccination protocol
- **No FCoV vaccine** (FIP vaccine) - not recommended, can interfere with diagnosis

## Ethical Considerations in Neonatal/Pediatric FIP

### When is Treatment Appropriate?

**Consider:**
- **Kitten's Quality of Life:** Is treatment causing suffering?
- **Prognosis:** Realistic chance of success?
- **Owner Resources:** Financial and time commitment feasible?
- **Kitten's Resilience:** Strong enough to tolerate treatment?

**Treatment May NOT Be Appropriate:**
- Neonates <300g (too fragile)
- Severe neurological FIP in tiny kitten
- Owner cannot commit to protocol
- Suffering outweighs potential benefit

**Euthanasia May Be Humane:**
- Tiny size, poor prognosis
- Owner cannot provide intensive care
- Quality of life very poor
- No judgment in this decision

### Discussing Difficult Decisions

**For Very Young/Fragile Kittens:**

*"[Kitten's name] is very small and fragile. While treatment is possible, it will be very intensive and success is not guaranteed. I want you to have realistic expectations. We can try if you're willing, but it's also okay to choose euthanasia if that feels like the kinder choice for [name]."*

**Supporting Owner's Decision:**
- **If treat:** "We'll give [name] the best chance possible."
- **If euthanasia:** "You're preventing suffering. That's a loving decision."

## Summary: Keys to Pediatric FIP Management

### ✅ Critical Success Factors:

1. **Accurate Dosing:**
   - Gram-scale weight measurement
   - Precise volume calculation
   - Weekly dose adjustments for growth

2. **Careful Administration:**
   - Appropriate needles and technique
   - Site rotation strict
   - Monitor for complications

3. **Aggressive Nutritional Support:**
   - High-calorie food
   - Appetite stimulants if needed
   - Feeding tube if necessary
   - Monitor growth

4. **Close Monitoring:**
   - Weekly weights and exams
   - Regular bloodwork
   - Watch for complications unique to kittens

5. **Owner Commitment:**
   - Daily injections for months
   - Frequent vet visits
   - Financial investment
   - Intensive care for very young kittens

### ⚠️ Common Pitfalls to Avoid:

- ❌ Failing to increase dose as kitten grows
- ❌ Inaccurate dose measurement (volume too small to measure accurately)
- ❌ Inadequate site rotation in small kitten
- ❌ Insufficient caloric intake
- ❌ Overlooking concurrent conditions (parasites, infections)
- ❌ Using maternal antibody tests for diagnosis
- ❌ Underestimating financial commitment as kitten grows
