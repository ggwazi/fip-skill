# Laboratory Reference Guide for FIP

> **Purpose:** Comprehensive guide to interpreting laboratory results in FIP diagnosis and monitoring
> **Audience:** Veterinarians managing FIP cases
> **Evidence Base:** ABCD Guidelines 2024, published diagnostic studies, UC Davis protocols

---

## Overview

Laboratory testing plays a crucial role in FIP diagnosis and treatment monitoring. While no single test definitively diagnoses FIP (except histopathology), a constellation of laboratory findings supports the diagnosis and tracks treatment response.

**This guide covers:**
- Complete Blood Count (CBC) interpretation
- Chemistry panel interpretation with focus on proteins
- Effusion analysis and Rivalta test
- Advanced diagnostic testing
- Monitoring bloodwork trends during treatment
- When to adjust therapy based on laboratory changes

---

## Complete Blood Count (CBC) in FIP

###Anemia

**Finding:** Non-regenerative anemia (mild to moderate)

**Typical Values:**
- HCT: 20-30% (normal: 30-45%)
- RBC: decreased
- Reticulocytes: normal to low (non-regenerative)

**Interpretation:**
- **Common in FIP (50-70% of cases)**
- Anemia of chronic inflammation
- Bone marrow suppression from inflammatory cytokines
- Severity correlates with disease chronicity

**Differential Diagnosis:**
- FIP (non-regenerative)
- FeLV (regenerative or non-regenerative)
- Chronic kidney disease (non-regenerative)
- Hemolytic anemia (regenerative)

**Monitoring During Treatment:**
- Should improve gradually
- HCT increases over weeks to months
- Persistent anemia after 4-8 weeks warrants investigation

---

### Leukocyte Changes

#### Lymphopenia

**Finding:** Low lymphocyte count

**Typical Values:**
- Lymphocytes: <1500/μL (normal: 1500-7000/μL)
- Often <1000/μL in FIP

**Interpretation:**
- **Very common in FIP (50-75% of cases)**
- Virus affects lymphoid tissue
- Immune dysregulation
- Stress response

**Diagnostic Value:**
- Supportive of FIP but not specific
- Also seen in stress, FeLV, other viral diseases

**During Treatment:**
- Should normalize as cat improves
- Persistent lymphopenia concerning for non-response

#### Neutrophilia

**Finding:** Elevated neutrophil count

**Typical Values:**
- Neutrophils: >12,000/μL (normal: 2500-12,000/μL)
- Often 15,000-30,000/μL

**Interpretation:**
- Inflammatory response
- Common in FIP, especially with effusion
- Stress response contributes

**Pattern:**
- Often mature neutrophilia (non-regenerative left shift rare)
- Toxic changes uncommon unless concurrent bacterial infection

#### Monocytosis

**Finding:** Elevated monocyte count

**Typical Values:**
- Monocytes: >800/μL (normal: 0-800/μL)

**Interpretation:**
- Chronic inflammation
- Phagocytic response
- Seen in approximately 25-40% of FIP cases

---

### Thrombocytopenia

**Finding:** Low platelet count

**Typical Values:**
- Platelets: <200,000/μL (normal: 200,000-500,000/μL)
- Usually mild (150,000-200,000)
- Severe thrombocytopenia (<50,000) less common

**Interpretation:**
- Immune-mediated destruction
- Consumption in inflammatory process
- Seen in 15-30% of FIP cases

**Clinical Significance:**
- Rarely causes bleeding unless severe
- Monitor during treatment

---

### Classic CBC Pattern in FIP

**Typical FIP CBC:**
- ✓ Non-regenerative anemia (mild to moderate)
- ✓ Lymphopenia (common)
- ✓ Neutrophilia (common)
- ✓ Monocytosis (variable)
- ✓ Thrombocytopenia (mild, variable)

**Important:** No CBC pattern is pathognomonic for FIP. Use in conjunction with other findings.

## Chemistry Panel in FIP

### Protein Abnormalities (Most Important for FIP)

#### Hyperglobulinemia

**Finding:** Elevated total globulins

**Typical Values:**
- Globulins: >5.0 g/dL (normal: 2.6-5.1 g/dL)
- Often >6.0 g/dL, sometimes >7.0 g/dL in FIP
- Severe cases: >8.0 g/dL

**Interpretation:**
- **Hallmark of FIP**
- Polyclonal gammopathy
- Excessive antibody production (ineffective against FIP virus)
- Seen in 70-90% of FIP cases

**Measurement:**
- Total globulin = Total Protein - Albumin
- If not reported, calculate manually

**During Treatment:**
- Should decrease over weeks
- Normalization indicates successful treatment
- Failure to decrease suggests treatment failure

#### Hypoalbuminemia

**Finding:** Low albumin

**Typical Values:**
- Albumin: <2.5 g/dL (normal: 2.5-3.9 g/dL)
- Often 1.5-2.3 g/dL in FIP with effusion

**Interpretation:**
- Loss into effusion (wet FIP)
- Decreased production (liver involvement)
- Increased catabolism (inflammation)
- Seen in 50-80% of wet FIP, less common in dry FIP

**Severity:**
- Mild: 2.0-2.5 g/dL
- Moderate: 1.5-2.0 g/dL
- Severe: <1.5 g/dL

**Clinical Impact:**
- Contributes to effusion formation
- Affects oncotic pressure
- Severe hypoalbuminemia worsens prognosis slightly

#### Albumin:Globulin (A:G) Ratio

**Calculation:** A:G Ratio = Albumin ÷ Globulin

**Normal Values:** 0.6-1.5 (varies slightly by lab)

**FIP Cutoff:** **<0.8 highly suspicious for FIP**

**Interpretation:**

| A:G Ratio | Interpretation |
|-----------|----------------|
| >1.0 | FIP unlikely (but not ruled out) |
| 0.8-1.0 | Equivocal; consider FIP along with other findings |
| 0.6-0.8 | Suspicious for FIP |
| <0.6 | Highly suspicious for FIP |
| <0.4 | Very strongly suggestive of FIP |

**Diagnostic Value:**
- **Most useful single biochemical parameter**
- Sensitivity: ~70% (30% of FIP cases have A:G >0.8)
- Specificity: ~80-90% (other chronic inflammatory diseases can cause low A:G)

**Examples:**

**Case 1:** Albumin 2.0, Globulin 7.5
- A:G = 2.0 ÷ 7.5 = 0.27 → **Highly suspicious for FIP**

**Case 2:** Albumin 2.8, Globulin 4.2
- A:G = 2.8 ÷ 4.2 = 0.67 → **Suspicious for FIP**

**Case 3:** Albumin 3.0, Globulin 3.5
- A:G = 3.0 ÷ 3.5 = 0.86 → **Equivocal; need more data**

**During Treatment:**
- Should increase over weeks (globulins decrease, albumin stabilizes/increases)
- **A:G normalizing (>0.8-1.0) indicates successful treatment**
- Failure to improve suggests treatment inadequate

**Monitoring Frequency:**
- Baseline, then every 2-4 weeks during treatment
- Track trend, not single values

### Bilirubin Elevation

**Finding:** Elevated total bilirubin

**Typical Values:**
- Total bilirubin: >0.3 mg/dL (normal: 0.1-0.3 mg/dL)
- Often 0.5-3.0 mg/dL in FIP
- Severe cases: >3.0 mg/dL (icterus clinically visible)

**Interpretation:**
- Hepatic involvement (common in FIP)
- Cholestasis from granulomatous hepatitis
- Hemolysis (less common)
- Seen in 40-60% of FIP cases, especially wet FIP

**Clinical Significance:**
- Icterus visible if >2.5-3.0 mg/dL
- Worsens prognosis slightly but many icteric cats still respond to treatment

**During Treatment:**
- Should decrease within 2-4 weeks
- Normalization indicates liver recovery

---

### Liver Enzymes

**Findings:** Variable elevation

**ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase):**
- Normal: 10-100 U/L
- FIP: Often 100-300 U/L (mild to moderate elevation)
- Occasionally >500 U/L

**AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase):**
- Normal: 10-100 U/L
- FIP: Often 50-200 U/L

**ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase):**
- Normal: 10-90 U/L
- FIP: Variable (may be normal or elevated)

**GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase):**
- More specific for cholestasis than ALP in cats
- May be elevated with biliary involvement

**Interpretation:**
- Hepatocellular damage (ALT, AST)
- Cholestasis (ALP, GGT, bilirubin)
- Common in FIP due to granulomatous hepatitis
- Not specific for FIP

**During Treatment:**
- Monitor if elevated (every 2-4 weeks)
- Should decrease as FIP resolves
- Persistent elevation: consider hepatotoxicity of treatment (rare with GS-441524)

---

### Renal Parameters

**BUN and Creatinine:**

**Usually Normal in FIP** unless:
- Renal form of dry FIP (granulomas in kidneys)
- Dehydration
- Concurrent kidney disease

**Elevated in ~20-30% of Dry FIP:**
- BUN >30 mg/dL
- Creatinine >1.6 mg/dL

**Interpretation:**
- Check for concurrent CKD (especially older cats)
- Assess hydration status
- If elevated, monitor closely during treatment

**During Treatment:**
- GS-441524 is renally excreted
- Monitor renal function every 2-4 weeks
- Ensure adequate hydration

---

### Electrolytes

**Usually Normal or Mildly Abnormal:**

**Potassium:**
- May be decreased if not eating

**Sodium/Chloride:**
- Usually normal

**Ionized Calcium:**
- Typically normal
- Check if neurological signs present

## Protein Electrophoresis

**Purpose:** Characterize globulin elevation

**Method:**
- Separates proteins into fractions
- Albumin, α1, α2, β, γ globulins

**Typical FIP Pattern:**
- **Polyclonal gammopathy** (broad-based increase in γ region)
- α2 globulins often elevated (inflammation)
- Albumin decreased

**Versus Other Diseases:**
- **Monoclonal gammopathy** (sharp peak): Myeloma, lymphoma → NOT FIP
- Helps differentiate FIP from lymphoma

**Diagnostic Value:**
- Supportive of FIP
- Not specific (other chronic infections cause polyclonal gammopathy)
- Not always necessary if A:G ratio clearly low

## Effusion Analysis

### Appearance

**Visual Inspection:**
- **Color:** Straw-yellow to gold (classic)
- **Clarity:** Clear to slightly cloudy, viscous
- **Texture:** Sticky, thick (high protein)

**FIP-Typical:**
- Clear to slightly turbulent
- Golden-yellow
- Viscous/thick

**Other Conditions:**
- **Septic peritonitis:** Cloudy, foul-smelling
- **Lymphoma:** Variable, often pink-tinged
- **CHF:** Clear, thin (transudative)

### Rivalta Test

**Purpose:** Rapid bedside test for FIP

**Procedure:**
1. Fill test tube with distilled water (5-7 mL)
2. Add 1 drop of glacial acetic acid (or white vinegar)
3. Mix gently
4. Add 1 drop of effusion on surface
5. Observe

**Positive Rivalta:**
- Drop maintains shape, sinks intact
- Forms jellyfish-like appearance
- Indicates high protein content

**Negative Rivalta:**
- Drop dissipates immediately
- Does not maintain shape

**Interpretation:**
- **Positive** → Highly suggestive of FIP (Sensitivity ~90%, Specificity ~75%)
- **Negative** → FIP less likely but not ruled out

**Limitations:**
- Positive in other exudates (bacterial peritonitis, some lymphomas)
- Operator-dependent
- Use in conjunction with other tests

### Protein Content

**Measurement:** Total protein in effusion

**Normal Transudate:** <2.5 g/dL

**FIP Typical:** **>3.5 g/dL**

**Interpretation:**

| Protein | Interpretation |
|---------|----------------|
| <2.5 g/dL | Transudate (CHF, hypoalbuminemia) → Not FIP |
| 2.5-3.5 g/dL | Modified transudate (variable causes) |
| >3.5 g/dL | Exudate → Consider FIP (if Rivalta+) |
| >5.0 g/dL | Very high protein → FIP most likely |

**Highest Diagnostic Value:**
- Protein >3.5 g/dL + Positive Rivalta = FIP very likely

### Cell Count and Differential

**Total Nucleated Cell Count (TNCC):**

**FIP Typical:** **<5,000 cells/μL** (often 1,000-3,000)

**Interpretation:**
- FIP is a **low-cellularity exudate** (unusual combination)
- High protein + low cells = classic FIP

**If TNCC >10,000:**
- Consider septic peritonitis (bacterial)
- Consider lymphoma
- FIP less likely but not ruled out

**Cell Differential:**

**FIP Typical:**
- Neutrophils: 50-90%
- Macrophages: 10-40%
- Lymphocytes: <10%

**Classic FIP Cytology:**
- Non-degenerate neutrophils (no bacteria)
- Activated macrophages
- Few lymphocytes
- High protein background

**Red Flags for Other Diagnoses:**
- **Degenerate neutrophils + bacteria:** Septic peritonitis
- **Predominant lymphocytes (>50%):** Lymphoma
- **Many atypical cells:** Lymphoma or carcinoma

### PCR on Effusion

**RT-PCR for FCoV RNA:**

**Sensitivity:** ~80-90% (if FIP with effusion)

**Specificity:** ~95-99% (if done on effusion, not blood/feces)

**Interpretation:**

| Result | Interpretation |
|--------|----------------|
| Positive | Highly supportive of FIP diagnosis |
| Negative | Does NOT rule out FIP (10-20% false negatives) |

**Important:**
- PCR on effusion is highly specific
- PCR on blood or feces is NOT diagnostic (many cats positive without FIP)
- Positive effusion PCR + typical effusion characteristics = FIP very likely

**Quantitative PCR:**
- Some labs report viral load
- Higher viral loads more consistent with FIP
- Cut-offs vary by laboratory

## Advanced Diagnostic Testing

### Immunofluorescence/Immunohistochemistry

**Purpose:** Detect FCoV antigen in macrophages

**Sample:** Effusion (cytology preparation) or tissue biopsy

**Method:**
- Immunostaining for FCoV antigen
- Requires specialized laboratory

**Interpretation:**
- **Positive:** Highly specific for FIP (>95%)
- **Negative:** Does not rule out FIP (sensitivity ~60-80%)

**Use:**
- Confirmatory test when available
- Biopsy provides gold standard (histopathology + immunostaining)

### AGP (α1-Acid Glycoprotein)

**Purpose:** Acute phase protein marker

**Measurement:** ELISA on serum or effusion

**FIP:**
- Elevated in most FIP cases
- Not specific (elevated in many inflammatory conditions)

**Diagnostic Value:**
- Supports FIP in context of other findings
- Not widely available
- Not essential for diagnosis

### Serum Coronavirus Antibody Titers

**Important: NOT USEFUL for FIP diagnosis**

**Problems:**
- High titers seen in cats with and without FIP
- Low titers do not rule out FIP
- Cannot distinguish FCoV from FIPV

**Recommendation:**
- **Do not use for diagnosis**
- May be used in research or specific screening contexts
- Misleading for clinicians and clients

## Monitoring Bloodwork During Treatment

### Baseline Testing

**Before Starting Treatment:**
- CBC
- Full chemistry panel (including total protein, albumin, globulin, A:G ratio)
- Abdominal ultrasound (if wet FIP, to track effusion)

### Treatment Monitoring Schedule

**Weeks 1-2:**
- Clinical monitoring (owner at home)
- No bloodwork needed unless clinical concerns

**Week 4:**
- CBC, chemistry panel
- **Look for:**
  - Globulins starting to decrease
  - A:G ratio starting to increase
  - Albumin stabilizing or increasing
  - Anemia improving
  - Lymphopenia resolving

**Week 8:**
- CBC, chemistry panel
- Ultrasound if wet FIP (effusion should be resolved or nearly resolved)
- **Look for:**
  - Clear trend toward normal values
  - Continued improvement in A:G ratio

**Week 12+:**
- CBC, chemistry panel
- **Goal:** All parameters normalizing

**End of Treatment:**
- CBC, chemistry panel
- **Criteria for Stopping Treatment:**
  - A:G ratio normalized (>0.8-1.0)
  - Globulins normal (<5.0 g/dL)
  - CBC normalized
  - Clinical signs resolved for 4+ weeks

**Post-Treatment:**
- Recheck at 3, 6, 12 months
- Watch for relapse

### Interpreting Trends

**Successful Treatment:**
- ✓ Globulins decrease progressively
- ✓ A:G ratio increases progressively
- ✓ Albumin stabilizes/increases
- ✓ Anemia resolves
- ✓ Lymphopenia resolves
- ✓ Bilirubin decreases (if elevated)

**Treatment Failure:**
- ❌ Globulins remain elevated or increase
- ❌ A:G ratio remains low or decreases
- ❌ Albumin continues to decline
- ❌ Persistent anemia/lymphopenia
- ❌ Clinical signs not improving

**Plateau:**
- Some parameters improving, others not
- → Consider dose increase, formulation change, or extended treatment

## When to Adjust Treatment Based on Labs

### Dose Increase Indicated:

**Clinical + Laboratory Criteria:**
- Week 4-6: No improvement in A:G ratio
- Week 8: Globulins not trending downward
- Clinical signs not improving
- Weight gain plateaued

**Action:**
- Increase dose by 25-50%
- Recheck bloodwork in 2-3 weeks

### Formulation Change Indicated:

**Criteria:**
- Oral medication + poor laboratory response
- Suspect bioavailability issue

**Action:**
- Switch to injectable
- Recheck bloodwork in 2-4 weeks

### Extended Treatment Indicated:

**Criteria:**
- Week 12: Improving but not normalized
- A:G ratio better but still <0.8
- Clinical signs resolved but bloodwork not normal

**Action:**
- Continue treatment beyond standard 12 weeks
- Recheck monthly until normalized

### Treatment Failure Suspected:

**Criteria:**
- Week 6-8: No laboratory improvement
- A:G ratio worsening
- Clinical deterioration

**Action:**
- Reassess diagnosis
- Consider refractory case protocols
- Discuss prognosis with owner

### Decision Tree: Is It FIP?

**High Suspicion:**
- A:G ratio <0.6 + compatible clinical signs
- Effusion: Rivalta+ + Protein >3.5 g/dL + Positive PCR
- Hyperglobulinemia + lymphopenia + anemia + compatible signs

**Moderate Suspicion:**
- A:G ratio 0.6-0.8 + compatible clinical signs
- Effusion: Rivalta+ + Protein >3.5 g/dL (no PCR done)
- Some laboratory abnormalities + compatible signs

**Low Suspicion:**
- A:G ratio >0.8
- Effusion: Rivalta negative OR Protein <3.0 g/dL
- Few laboratory abnormalities

**Not FIP:**
- Effusion with degenerate neutrophils and bacteria
- Monoclonal gammopathy on electrophoresis
- A:G ratio normal + no other FIP findings
